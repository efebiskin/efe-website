Editor's letter

Noir began as a private question — what does taste look like when it is no longer scarce? We assembled this issue against the soft pressure of unlimited image, unlimited form, unlimited reference. Our editors agreed only on this: that beauty, generated or otherwise, deserves the same patience we have always offered the rare.

This first issue is a study, not a verdict. We invite you to read slowly. — M. Lavigne, Founding Editor.

Essay 02

On Generated
Beauty.

By M. Lavigne. Published Spring 2026. 4,200 words. Estimated read · 18 minutes.

For a long time, a single image of a couture house's spring collection might travel the world for a season — passed between editors, copied onto contact sheets, faxed to retail teams who pinned it above their cash registers. The image was rare and so the image mattered.

That economy is gone. Image is now infinite, instantaneous, and almost free. The question this issue tries to hold, with as little drama as possible, is whether beauty survives that change.

One school answers no. They argue that scarcity is the only honest substrate of taste — that a thing's value rests on its difficulty, and a generated image, by being effortless, is therefore weightless. We respect this position and we believe it is incomplete.

The novelist Calvino, writing in Six Memos for the Next Millennium, named lightness as a virtue — not weightlessness, but lightness with content. We borrow the distinction.

The other school answers, more interestingly, that beauty was never about rarity at all. That what rarity gave us — and what it now removes — was a kind of forced patience. When the image was rare, we had no choice but to dwell on it. We turned it over in our hands. We argued about it across decades. We forgot it and rediscovered it.

A note on patience.

What we lost, then, was not beauty. We lost the institutional architecture that made us look slowly. The editorial calendar. The print quarterly. The held breath of a runway show before the lights came up. The fact that, before the internet, you could not see a Margiela collection until the magazines arrived.

NOIR begins with a small wager: that the architecture of patience can be rebuilt, voluntarily, around any image — generated or otherwise. That a quarterly publication, slow and curated, is not a nostalgic gesture. It is the most modern editorial decision available to us.

What this issue does.

The remaining pages of Issue 01 are arranged accordingly. We present three editorials, an interview, and a reference — each of them designed to be looked at twice. We have not labelled which images are generated and which are photographed. We invite you, instead, to notice what changes in your attention when you cannot tell.

Read carefully. — M.L.
